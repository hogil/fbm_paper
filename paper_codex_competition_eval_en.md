# Internal Paper Competition Evaluation Notes (English)

## 1) Excellence

The greatest strength of this work is that it establishes an integrated system that can pre-generate, store, browse, and analyze full-resolution Failbit Maps at scale, which was not feasible in the previous environment. Existing workflows relied on Measure-based analysis and chip-level clustering, and even basic map inspection was severely limited because on-demand conversion from equipment logs suffered from major speed and memory constraints. In practice, only around 48 maps could be checked at once, making full-wafer analysis unrealistic.

This study removes that bottleneck at the infrastructure level. By applying Cython to the repetitive hex-to-grade conversion loop, data transformation speed was improved by about 100x. By leveraging the domain characteristic that Failbit Maps are composed of a small number of discrete grade colors, the study also applied 32-palette-indexed PNG and reduced storage size by about 75%. This made it possible to pre-generate and store more than 2,000 wafer maps per day on a 1-hour cycle and to support large-scale browsing through a Web App.

The Web App also demonstrates strong usability under real working conditions. It can load 50 maps in about one second, and even when 5,000 images are selected, engineers can continue scrolling and browsing without meaningful delay. In addition, the palette-indexed representation allows engineers to change grade colors and chip-boundary colors interactively by role or analysis purpose. With a conventional RGB-based approach, such customization would require pixel-wise replacement across the full image. In the palette-based approach, only the palette entries need to be changed, so user-specific visualization can be reflected immediately in the UI. This is a practical and efficient design that improves both storage efficiency and interactive performance.

On the AI side, the study introduced a selective two-stage `CNN -> ROI-YOLO` structure for Known defect classification and improved performance from a single-model F1-score of 0.87 to a final 0.95. For Unknown defect detection, instead of the conventional industry approach based on chip-level measurements and clustering at roughly 1,000 chips per wafer, the study applied contrastive learning directly to pixel-level Failbit Maps at roughly 10 million pixels per wafer. This shifts both the analysis resolution and the learning paradigm. In production-data validation, 7 out of 13 automatically suggested candidates were confirmed as true new defect groups.

The data pipeline and Web App are already being operated daily on a DRAM production line without failures. Combined with the lightweight backbone choice and the selective second-stage design, the system is scalable to much larger daily volumes and has a clear path toward expansion to additional product families.

## 2) Originality

The originality of this work lies in the fact that it advances both major axes of semiconductor defect analysis, Known classification and Unknown discovery, beyond the level of typical industrial practice, and integrates them into a single operational pipeline.

The most original part of the Unknown path is the simultaneous shift in both data resolution and learning paradigm. Conventional industry practice relied on clustering chip-level measurements at roughly 1,000 chips per wafer. That approach had inherently low spatial resolution and limited expressive power for subtle or previously unseen defect patterns. In contrast, this study applies contrastive learning directly to ultra-high-resolution Failbit Maps with roughly 10 million pixels per wafer. This moves the task from low-resolution clustering to representation learning on full map structures. In addition, the study does not stop at generic contrastive learning. It introduces grid structured local sampling so that the model reflects the zone-based interpretation used by semiconductor engineers, where defect location on the wafer is itself an important clue for root-cause analysis. The method partitions the wafer into grids and encourages samples from the same zone to be learned as similar, embedding domain knowledge directly into the learning process.

The originality of the Known path lies in the hierarchical and selective `CNN -> ROI-YOLO` architecture. In industrial data, some defect classes have very similar wafer-level spatial distributions even though the actual chip-level defect patterns are different. A single CNN therefore has an inherent limitation on such confusing classes. This work addresses that by letting a first-stage ConvNeXtV2 classify the overall wafer-level pattern, then invoking a second-stage YOLO only for low-confidence samples. Grad-CAM is used to identify the defect-concentrated region, and that ROI is directly passed into YOLO, which then inspects the chip-level pattern and placement in more detail. The decision process therefore expands hierarchically from full-wafer distribution to ROI to chip-level pattern, and the first model's attention signal becomes the second model's input. This is fundamentally different from a single-model solution or a loose ensemble.

There is also originality in the implementation strategy. The study translated a heavy repeated transformation task into a compiled Cython loop for roughly 100x acceleration, and used palette-indexed PNG not only to reduce storage by roughly 75% but also to enable real-time UI color customization for different engineering use cases. This is a smart design choice that solves both technical and operational requirements at once.

Finally, the study delivers an innovative practical result: among 13 automatically suggested candidate groups from real production data, 7 were confirmed as true new defect groups. This demonstrates the feasibility of a human-in-the-loop workflow in which AI proposes likely new defects before engineers would have recognized them through conventional analysis.

## 3) Validity of Evidence

This study was designed, implemented, and validated on real Samsung DRAM production data rather than academic benchmark datasets, which makes the evidence especially strong. For Known defect classification, 1,500 labeled samples across 16 classes were prepared by domain engineers and split into train and test sets using a 4:1 stratified split so that class proportions were preserved and the evaluation remained fair.

The experiments were structured so that the contribution of each design decision could be measured separately. Using Weighted F1 as the metric, five backbones were compared under the same conditions: ViT (0.81), Swin (0.84), EffNetV2 (0.85), MaxViT (0.87), and ConvNeXtV2 (0.87). Since MaxViT and ConvNeXtV2 achieved the same accuracy, ConvNeXtV2 was selected because it was more efficient for the intended operational environment, with parameters reduced from 119.5M to 88.6M and FLOPs reduced from 74.2G to 45.1G. After that, Optuna was used to optimize fine-tuning hyperparameters such as learning rate, weight decay, focal loss, and class weight, improving F1 to 0.92. Adding ROI-YOLO for low-confidence samples further improved the final score to 0.95. The stage-by-stage gain, 0.87 -> 0.92 -> 0.95, is therefore clearly measured and attributable.

The data pipeline and Web App performance were also verified directly. Applying Cython improved the hex-to-grade conversion speed by about 100x. Using palette-indexed PNG reduced storage size by about 75%. In the operational environment, the system showed strong practical performance, including loading around 50 maps in about one second and supporting smooth browsing even when 5,000 images were selected.

For Unknown defect discovery, the study trained on 10,000 production maps collected over five days and evaluated on a fully separate one-day set of 2,000 maps, thereby reproducing the intended real deployment scenario. The model suggested 13 candidate groups, and domain engineers reviewed all of them. Seven were confirmed as true new defect groups, while the remaining six were interpreted as lot-level warning noise or patterns that did not lead to actual chip defects. This combination of quantitative output and direct expert validation provides stronger evidence than a purely laboratory-style evaluation.

The logic of the research is also consistent throughout: it starts from real operational limits such as the 48-map bottleneck, chip-level clustering limitations, and manual reading, then proposes specific corresponding methods such as pre-generation and storage, `CNN -> ROI-YOLO` two-stage classification, pixel-level contrastive learning, and grid structured local sampling, and finally validates them through both numerical results and production use.

## 4) Impact

The greatest impact of this work is that it changes the foundation of semiconductor defect analysis operations. A workflow that previously allowed only about 48 maps to be checked at once has been transformed into a system that can pre-generate, store, and provide large-scale browsing for more than 2,000 wafers per day on a 1-hour cycle. Engineers now access the Web App every day and review Failbit Maps with practical speed, including roughly one-second loading for 50 maps and smooth browsing even for thousands of images. The scope of analysis has therefore expanded from partial sampling to full monitoring.

The Web App also improves actual daily work. Engineers can view large volumes of maps, compare defect patterns, and use UI-level convenience features such as interactive color customization for grades and chip boundaries. This does not merely add convenience; it removes repetitive manual inspection burden and gives engineers the information they actually need in a usable form.

From the AI perspective, the Known path achieved F1 0.95 through the selective `CNN -> ROI-YOLO` design, and the Unknown path identified 13 candidates from production data, 7 of which were confirmed as true new defects. This means that the workflow can move from reacting after yield issues become visible to proactively surfacing possible new defect patterns in advance. Given the cost impact of yield loss, earlier detection can make a substantial business contribution.

In terms of broader applicability, the current deployment is on a DRAM-specific product, but the same architecture can be extended to other memory products such as HBM, LPDDR, and NAND wherever Failbit Maps are generated. More broadly, the combined Known/Unknown analysis framework and the pixel-level contrastive-learning approach can also be applied to other image-based semiconductor analysis tasks, including SEM images or overlay maps, and even to other industries where large-scale image quality analysis is required.

The system is also sustainable and self-improving. It is already under real operation, and future versions can directly integrate model outputs and candidate groups into the production Web App. When Unknown candidates are validated by engineers and later registered as Known classes, the classification scope expands automatically. As more production data accumulates, the models can be retrained and improved continuously. In that sense, the work does not end at deployment; it creates the basis for a long-term improvement loop and opens the door to future multimodal extensions that combine pixel-level Failbit Maps with electrical measurements such as FTN and QTN.
